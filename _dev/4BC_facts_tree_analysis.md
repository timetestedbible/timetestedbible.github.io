# Analysis: 4BC Facts Tree vs. Book Claims

## Key Disagreements

### 1. HEROD'S DEATH DATE

**4BC Tree Claims:**
- H4: Partial lunar eclipse March 13, 4 BC
- H15: Herod's death Spring 4 BC (post-eclipse, pre-Passover)
- H9a: Philip dies 20th year of Tiberius (AD 33/34) → supports 4 BC

**Book Claims:**
- January 10, 1 BC **total** lunar eclipse (blood-red, 1 hour 39 minutes)
- March 13, 4 BC eclipse was "faint partial" at 3 AM "when few would notice it"
- Herod's death Spring 1 BC
- 114-day post-eclipse window to Passover (fits timeline)
- Only 29 days between 4 BC eclipse and Passover (too short for timeline)

**Book's Evidence:**
- Seven independent proofs converge on 1 BC:
  1. January 10, 1 BC total lunar eclipse (dramatic, visible)
  2. 114-day post-eclipse window to Passover
  3. Exact 27 years "to the day" from Pompey's 63 BC capture to Herod's 36 BC conquest
  4. Sabbatical-year match from 1406 BC Jordan cycle
  5. Age ~70 at death (from 15 in 55/54 BC → born ~70/69 BC)
  6. 107 high priests from Herod's "days" (38/35 BC) to 70 AD
  7. Early Latin MSS: Philip dies 22nd year (AD 35/36) after 37 years from 1 BC

**Verdict:** Book provides multiple independent lines of evidence for 1 BC. 4BC tree relies on single eclipse and Greek MSS reading.

---

### 2. CAPTURE OF JERUSALEM DATE

**4BC Tree Claims:**
- H6: Summer 37 BC
- H2: Consuls Marcus Agrippa and Caninius Gallus = 37 BC
- H3: 185th Olympiad (40-36 BC)

**Book Claims:**
- **Fall 36 BC** (Day of Atonement, 10th of 7th month)
- Exact 27 years "to the day" from Pompey's 63 BC capture
- First de facto year starts spring 35 BC (spring-to-spring reckoning)
- Consulship reference can refer to start of siege, not conclusion
- 185th Olympiad ended before Day of Atonement in 36 BC, but siege started during it
- Sabbatical year match (7th year of cycle from 1406 BC)

**Book's Evidence:**
- Josephus: "Herod took Jerusalem on the very same day … after twenty-seven years' time"
- Both events on Day of Atonement (10th of 7th month)
- 27 years from 63 BC = 36 BC (not 37 BC)
- 107 high priests calculation points to 38 BC regnal / 35 BC de facto start

**Verdict:** Book's "27 years to the day" is explicit and precise. 4BC tree's 37 BC requires dismissing this precision or reinterpreting it.

---

### 3. PHILIP'S DEATH / MANUSCRIPT READINGS

**4BC Tree Claims:**
- H9a: 20th year of Tiberius (Greek MSS) → AD 33/34 → 4 BC Herod death
- H9b: 22nd year of Tiberius (Latin MSS) → AD 35/36 → 1 BC Herod death (minority)
- H9a confidence: High (majority reading)
- H9b confidence: Moderate (older but minority)

**Book Claims:**
- **H8b (22nd year) is superior:**
  - 300-500 years older than Greek MSS
  - Unanimous in early Latin translations (4th-8th centuries)
  - Multiple independent geographic origins
  - Allows standard 1-year ante-dating (consistent with Archelaus, Antipas)
  - Fits 1 BC death without special pleading
- **H8a (20th year) requires:**
  - Zero-year ante-dating (unprecedented for Herodian rulers)
  - Special pleading to fit 4 BC death

**Book's Evidence:**
- "In every single metric the older 22nd year manuscripts win, hands down"
- All ~20 surviving early Latin copies unanimous
- Greek MSS are 9th-10th century (much later)

**Verdict:** Book provides detailed manuscript analysis showing 22nd year reading is older, unanimous, and fits patterns. 4BC tree prioritizes majority over age/unanimity.

### 4. FIRST FRUITS TIMING

**4BC Tree Claims:**
- H13: First Fruits variable (day after Sabbath during Unleavened Bread = first Sunday after Passover)
- S3: "Day after the Sabbath" = first Sunday after Passover
- Alternative: Fixed 16th Nisan (Sadducees, Josephus, Philo) - but not preferred
- S4: No consecutive 15th Sabbaths paradox (if First Fruits variable)
- S5: No First Fruits paradox (if First Fruits variable)

**Book Claims:**
- **First Fruits always on 16th** (S3 in book's dependency tree)
- Sources: Septuagint, Josephus, Philo, Joshua 5:10-12, Exodus narrative
- **S4: First Fruits is First Day of Week** (derived from Leviticus 23:15-16 requirement)
- **S5: Consecutive 15ths as Sabbaths** (mathematically impossible with continuous cycle)
- **S6: First Fruits Paradox** (counting 7 weeks from 16th doesn't land on "day after 7th Sabbath" with continuous cycle)

**Book's Evidence:**
- Leviticus 23:15-16 creates mathematical requirement: count from First Fruits for 7 complete sabbaths, ending on "day after the 7th sabbath"
- Under continuous cycle, this requires First Fruits to be on first day of week
- But First Fruits is always on 16th (proven independently)
- Creates paradox: in 6 of 7 years, 16th won't be first day of week with continuous cycle
- **Book explicitly addresses variable First Fruits theory:**
  - "The only way to escape this paradox is if first fruits is not always on the 16th but is the first Sunday after Passover and this is many people attempt to resolve it. But we have already seen how that breaks down with historical narratives in 1446 BC, 1406 BC, and 32 AD."
- Exodus travel/typology: Even if assuming variable First Fruits, narrative shows 16th was First Fruits, meaning 15th was Sabbath

**Verdict:** Book provides scriptural requirement (Leviticus 23:15-16) that creates paradox with continuous cycle. 4BC tree resolves paradox by making First Fruits variable, but book shows this contradicts historical evidence (Septuagint, Josephus, Philo, biblical examples).

### 5. CALENDAR SYSTEM (LUNAR SABBATH vs CONTINUOUS WEEK)

**4BC Tree Claims:**
- A3: Months begin at first visible crescent; weeks are continuous 7-day cycles
- H11: New moon month start (first crescent sighting)
- H12: Continuous 7-day week (Sabbath every 7th day, independent of moon)
- C1: Continuous weekly Sabbath (every 7th day, not lunar-tied)
- S4/S5: No paradoxes (if First Fruits variable)

**Book Claims:**
- **Lunar Sabbath system** (Sabbaths on 8th, 15th, 22nd, 29th of each month)
- Month starts with **full moon** (not first crescent)
- S1: Month starts with full moon (renewed moon = full moon)
- S5: Consecutive 15ths as Sabbaths (proves lunar sabbath)
- S6: First Fruits paradox (proves continuous cycle wrong)

**Book's Evidence:**
- S5: Consecutive 15ths mathematically impossible with continuous cycle
- S6: First Fruits paradox refutes continuous cycle
- Historical tests (1446 BC, 1406 BC, 32 AD) fail with continuous cycle
- Moving celestial dateline (resets with each new month)

**Verdict:** Fundamental disagreement. Book provides multiple independent proofs (S5, S6, historical tests) that continuous cycle fails. 4BC tree assumes continuous cycle and resolves paradoxes by making First Fruits variable.

### 6. HEROD'S AGE AT DEATH

**4BC Tree Claims:**
- Not explicitly addressed in foundational facts
- H15 mentions "about seventy" but doesn't test it

**Book Claims:**
- Herod was **15 years old** when appointed governor of Galilee in 55/54 BC
- Born ~70/69 BC
- Age ~70 at death → **1 BC death** (not 4 BC)
- 4 BC would make him at most 67 years old (contradicts Josephus)

**Book's Evidence:**
- Josephus: "This was done in the ninth year of Hyrcanus the high priest and ethnarch, in the month Panemus" (July) - 55/54 BC
- Josephus: "Antipater … made his son Herod governor of Galilee, when he was but fifteen years old"
- Josephus: "He was about seventy when he died" (Ant. 17.6.1 & War 1.33.1)
- 55/54 BC - 15 years = 70/69 BC birth
- 70/69 BC + 70 years = 1 BC/1 AD death

**Verdict:** Book provides clear calculation. 4BC tree doesn't address this evidence.

### 7. HEROD'S APPOINTMENT DATE

**4BC Tree Claims:**
- H5: Late 40 BC (after Brundisium Treaty)
- **Key Assumption**: Journey completed before winter closed Mediterranean Sea, despite documented obstacles
- Consular reference (40 BC) interpreted as marking appointment date

**Book Claims:**
- **After Nisan 1 in 39 BC** (around April/May)
- Spring 38 BC becomes first full regnal year
- Voyage delays explain 5-6 month gap
- Full moon calendar's delayed Shavuot extends journey timeline
- Dio Cassius supports 39 BC date
- **Consular reference marks journey start, not appointment completion**

**Book's Evidence:**
- **Journey Timeline** (from Extra Chapter 03):
  - Parthian invasion escalated around Shavuot (July 1, 40 BC with full moon calendar)
  - Overland escape via Masada, Petra, Rhinocolura to Alexandria (4-6 weeks) → arrival mid-August 40 BC
  - **Shipwreck and storm**: Josephus records "violent storm" causing loss of cargo, forcing detour to Rhodes (Antiquities 14.14.3)
  - **Refitting in Rhodes**: Building "three-decked ship" in war-damaged city (3+ months) → extends to late October/early November 40 BC
  - **Mare Clausum**: Mediterranean winter season (November-March) - voyages rare and perilous
  - **Result**: Herod likely wintered in Rhodes, pushing journey completion to spring 39 BC
- **Consular Reference Interpretation**:
  - Josephus: "obtained it on the hundred eighty fourth olympiad; when Caius Domitius Calvinus was consul the second time; and Caius Asinius Pollio [the first time]"
  - Reference pertains to **start of journey sequence** (Parthian invasion/flight), not appointment completion
  - Evidence: Narrative placement, "obtaining" language encompasses full process, no precise appointment timestamp
- Appian (Civil Wars 5.75) - Antony's reorganizations after treaty, winter 40/39 BC in Athens
- Dio Cassius (Roman History 49.22) - Herod's campaigns after Ventidius's 39-38 BC Parthian victories
- Senate vote could slip past Nisan 1 in April/May 39 BC amid bureaucratic delays

**The 4 BC Position's Burden:**
- Must prove Herod defied documented storm and shipwreck
- Completed refitting in less than 3 months
- Risked winter Mediterranean sailing (against all historical precedent)
- Completed journey before November 40 BC
- **All without a shred of direct evidence for such haste**

**Verdict:** Book provides detailed timeline with **direct evidence from Josephus** about journey obstacles. 4BC tree assumes late 40 BC based on **unproven assumption** about journey speed, requiring Herod to defy documented delays without evidence. The consular reference interpretation (journey start vs. appointment) is more consistent with Josephus's narrative style.

### 8. BATTLE OF ACTIUM - INDEPENDENT TEST OF REGNAL YEARS

**4BC Tree Claims:**
- H16: Regnal start 40 BC (from appointment)
- **Note**: Battle of Actium (31 BC) dated to "seventh year of the reign" creates 3-year discrepancy
- Alternative: Use inclusive de facto counting from 37 BC capture, but requires abandoning "reign" = regnal years

**Book Claims:**
- Battle of Actium (September 2, 31 BC) occurred during Herod's 7th regnal year
- **Book's Position (38 BC regnal start)**: Year 7 = Spring 32 BC to Spring 31 BC; Actium (Sept 2, 31 BC) is about **5 months into Year 8** - **5 month discrepancy** (imperfect but very close)
- **4 BC Position (40 BC regnal start)**: Year 7 = Spring 34 BC to Spring 33 BC; Actium (Sept 2, 31 BC) is in **Year 10** - **3 year discrepancy** (major error)

**Book's Evidence:**
- Josephus Antiquities 15.5.2: "Now at this time it was that the fight happened at Actium, between Octavius Caesar and Antony, in the seventh year of the reign of Herod"
- Uses **unqualified "reign" language** - meaning regnal years (from appointment), not de facto years (from capture)
- Battle of Actium is one of the most precisely dated events in ancient history (September 2, 31 BC)
- Provides **independent test** - doesn't depend on Herod's death, sons' reigns, or eclipses

**The 4 BC Position's Dilemma:**
- **Option A (regnal years from 40 BC)**: 3 years off - too large to be explained by approximation
- **Option B (inclusive de facto counting from 37 BC)**: Could work numerically, but requires **three deviations from convention**:
  1. Abandoning "reign" = regnal years (contradicts explicit language and Extra Chapter 1)
  2. Using de facto years instead (contradicts "reign" language)
  3. Using inclusive accession-year counting (deviation from standard practice)
- The book's position requires **zero deviations** - just 5 months off (likely approximation or boundary issue)

**Significance:**
- Book's position is **over 7x closer** to Josephus's statement (5 months vs. 3 years)
- Supports 38 BC regnal start (from 39 BC appointment)
- Exposes foundational assumption: 40 BC appointment requires unproven journey speed assumption
- Fits perfectly with overall chronology: 38 BC regnal start → Year 7 includes 31 BC

**Verdict:** Book's position fits Josephus's statement much better (5 months vs. 3 years off). The 4 BC position either has a major 3-year error or requires three deviations from convention to make it work. This provides independent validation of the 39 BC appointment date.

## SUMMARY OF CONFLICTS

### Critical Disagreements:

1. **Herod's Death:** 4 BC vs 1 BC
   - Book: 7 independent proofs for 1 BC
   - 4BC Tree: Single eclipse + Greek MSS

2. **Capture of Jerusalem:** 37 BC vs 36 BC
   - Book: "27 years to the day" explicit statement
   - 4BC Tree: Requires dismissing precision

3. **Eclipse:** March 13, 4 BC partial vs January 10, 1 BC total
   - Book: 1 BC eclipse dramatic, visible; 4 BC faint, 3 AM
   - 4BC Tree: Uses 4 BC partial eclipse

4. **Manuscript Priority:** Greek 20th year vs Latin 22nd year
   - Book: Latin MSS 300-500 years older, unanimous
   - 4BC Tree: Prioritizes Greek majority

5. **First Fruits:** Variable vs Always on 16th
   - Book: Always on 16th (multiple sources) + scriptural paradox
   - 4BC Tree: Variable to resolve paradoxes

6. **Calendar System:** Continuous week vs Lunar Sabbath
   - Book: Multiple proofs continuous cycle fails
   - 4BC Tree: Assumes continuous cycle, resolves paradoxes by making First Fruits variable

7. **Herod's Age:** Not addressed vs 70 years old
   - Book: Clear calculation from 15 years old in 55/54 BC
   - 4BC Tree: Doesn't test this evidence

8. **Herod's Appointment:** Late 40 BC vs After Nisan 1, 39 BC
   - Book: Journey timeline evidence (shipwreck, refitting, winter) from Josephus; consular reference marks journey start
   - 4BC Tree: Assumes rapid journey despite documented obstacles (unproven assumption)

9. **Battle of Actium:** 3-year discrepancy vs 5-month discrepancy
   - Book: Year 7 (31 BC) fits 38 BC regnal start (5 months into Year 8)
   - 4BC Tree: Year 7 (31 BC) is 3 years off from 40 BC regnal start, or requires 3 deviations from convention

## STRENGTH OF BOOK'S ARGUMENTS

The book provides:
- **Multiple independent lines of evidence** for each key claim
- **Explicit scriptural requirements** (Leviticus 23:15-16) that create mathematical contradictions
- **Manuscript analysis** showing older, unanimous witnesses
- **Historical tests** (1446 BC, 1406 BC, 32 AD) that fail with continuous cycle
- **Cross-validation** between different types of evidence (eclipses, regnal years, ages, sabbatical cycles)
- **Direct evidence from Josephus** about journey obstacles (shipwreck, refitting, winter)
- **Independent historical anchors** (Battle of Actium) that validate regnal year calculations

The 4BC tree:
- Relies on **single points of evidence** for key claims
- **Dismisses or reinterprets** explicit statements ("27 years to the day")
- **Prioritizes majority manuscripts** over older, unanimous ones
- **Resolves paradoxes** by changing historical facts (making First Fruits variable)
- **Doesn't address** several lines of evidence (Herod's age, 107 high priests, etc.)
- **Requires unproven assumptions** (rapid journey despite documented obstacles)
- **Has major discrepancies** (3-year error for Battle of Actium) or requires multiple deviations from convention

---

## CONCLUSION

The book's arguments are **stronger** because they:
1. Provide multiple independent witnesses for each claim
2. Preserve explicit statements rather than reinterpreting them
3. Use older, unanimous manuscript evidence
4. Create mathematical contradictions that refute alternative theories
5. Address more lines of evidence comprehensively
6. Use direct evidence from primary sources (Josephus's journey narrative) rather than assumptions
7. Provide independent historical anchors (Battle of Actium) that validate the chronology
8. Require zero deviations from convention (vs. 3 deviations needed for 4BC position)

The 4BC tree appears to:
1. Prioritize consensus/tradition over evidence
2. Require special pleading (zero-year ante-dating, dismissing "27 years to the day")
3. Resolve paradoxes by changing historical facts rather than calendar system
4. Miss or dismiss several independent lines of evidence
5. Rely on unproven assumptions (rapid journey despite documented obstacles)
6. Have major chronological discrepancies (3-year error for Battle of Actium) or require multiple deviations from convention
