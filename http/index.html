What is Î”T (Delta T)?

Î”T is the difference between Terrestrial Time (uniform, based on atomic clocks) and Universal Time (based on Earth's rotation). Earth's rotation is gradually slowing due to tidal friction, causing Î”T to grow over centuries.

Why does this matter for ancient calendars?

When calculating ancient lunar phases, the uncertainty in Î”T means we cannot know the exact moment a conjunction or full moon occurred. If the moon event was close to sunset, this uncertainty could shift whether Day 1 started on that evening or the next.

How Torah Portions Are Determined

ğŸ“œ The Annual Torah Cycle

The Torah (the five books of Moses) is divided into 54 weekly portions called parashot (singular: parashah). These portions are read sequentially throughout the year, completing the entire Torah in one annual cycle.

The cycle traditionally restarts on Shemini Atzeret (the 22nd day of the 7th month), when the final portion (Vezot HaBerachah) is read along with the beginning of Genesis, symbolizing the never-ending nature of Torah study.

ğŸŒ• Lunar Sabbath Cycle

On a lunar Sabbath calendar, Sabbaths always fall on fixed days of each lunar month: days 8, 15, 22, and 29. This gives exactly 4 Sabbaths per month.

How it works:

  • The cycle begins on Day 29 of Month 7 (the first Sabbath after Shemini Atzeret on day 22)
  • Portions progress through each subsequent Sabbath: Month 8 days 8, 15, 22, 29, then Month 9, etc.
  • In a 12-month year: approximately 46 regular Sabbaths for Torah reading
  • In a 13-month year: approximately 50 regular Sabbaths
  • Day 15 and Day 22 of Month 7 have special holiday readings (Sukkot and Shemini Atzeret)

Since the number of Sabbaths varies by year, some portions may be combined (doubled) in shorter years to complete the cycle by Shemini Atzeret.

ğŸª Saturday Sabbath Cycle

On a traditional Saturday Sabbath calendar, Sabbaths follow a fixed 7-day weekly cycle regardless of the lunar month.

How it works:

  • The cycle begins on Simchat Torah (the Saturday following Shemini Atzeret)
  • Portions progress week by week through the solar year
  • Most years have 52-54 Saturdays
  • When holidays fall on Saturday, special holiday readings replace the regular portion

The traditional Jewish calendar uses complex rules to determine when portions are combined or separated based on the Hebrew calendar year type.

The Priestly Cycle: An Introduction

The priestly cycle (also known as the priestly divisions, courses, or mishmarot in Hebrew) was the organized rotation system of 24 groups of priests (kohanim) who served in the Temple in Jerusalem. This structure ensured continuous, fair, and efficient Temple service over the course of the year.

Read more about the history...

Biblical Origin

The system is described in the Hebrew Bible in 1 Chronicles 24:1â€“19. King David, in preparation for the Temple that would be built by his son Solomon, divided the male descendants of Aaron (the first high priest) into 24 courses. With the help of Zadok (from the line of Eleazar) and Ahimelech (from the line of Ithamar), David cast lots to assign the order impartially. This was necessary because the descendants of Eleazar outnumbered those of Ithamar, resulting in 16 courses from Eleazar and 8 from Ithamar.

Each course served in the Temple for one full week (from Sabbath afternoon to the following Sabbath afternoon), with a midday handoff on the Sabbath. The rotation meant each group served approximately twice per year. All 24 courses participated together during the three major pilgrimage festivals: Passover, Shavuot, and Sukkot.

The names of the 24 courses are listed in 1 Chronicles 24:7â€“18, each named after its ancestral head who received the lot in that order.

When Did the Cycle Begin?

The priestly cycle began during the final years of King David's reign (approximately 1010â€“970 BCE), as part of his extensive preparations for the future Temple (1 Chronicles 24â€“26). The courses were formally implemented upon the dedication of the First Temple by Solomon (c. 951 BCE), as recorded in 2 Chronicles 8:12â€“14.

This rotation continued through the First Temple period, the Babylonian exile (with interruption during the destruction and exile), and was reinstated in the Second Temple era after its rebuilding in 516 BCE. It remained in active use until the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 CE.

Evidence for Continuity

The longevity of the cycle is demonstrated by several historical and textual anchors:

How are these dates calculated?

We know from ancient records that Course 1 (Jehoiarib) was serving when the Temple was destroyed on the 9th of Av, 70 CE. This gives us a fixed reference point to calculate backward and forward in time.

The Basic Idea

Think of it like a relay race with 24 teams. Each team runs for exactly one week, then passes the baton to the next team. After team 24 finishes, it goes back to team 1. This cycle repeats forever.

If we know which team was running on a specific date (the 9th of Av, 70 CE), we can count backward or forward to figure out which team was running on any other date in history.

For Saturday Sabbath Calendars

This is the simpler calculation:

  1. Find the Sunday that starts the week containing the 9th of Av, 70 CE (our reference point)
  2. Count how many days between that Sunday and the date you're interested in
  3. Divide by 7 to get the number of weeks
  4. Divide that by 24 (the number of priestly courses) and look at the remainder
  5. The remainder tells you which course is serving (0 = Course 1, 1 = Course 2, etc.)

For Lunar Sabbath Calendars

This is trickier because lunar months don't have a fixed number of days (they average about 29.53 days):

  1. Find the 9th of Av, 70 CE on the lunar calendar (our reference point)
  2. Count the days between the reference and your target date
  3. Divide by 29.53 to get the number of lunar months
  4. Multiply by 4 (since there are 4 weeks per lunar month)
  5. Adjust for which week of the month you're in (days 2-8, 9-15, 16-22, or 23-29)
  6. Divide by 24 and use the remainder to find the course

Why Different Calendars Give Different Answers

The 9th of Av falls on different Gregorian dates depending on which calendar system you use (full moon vs. dark moon vs. crescent, morning vs. evening day start, etc.). Since our reference point changes, the entire calculation shifts, which is why different calendar theories show different priestly courses for the same day.
